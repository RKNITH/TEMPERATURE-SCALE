Temperature and Its Measurement

Theory of Temperature

Temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy of the particles in a system. It is an important concept in physics and everyday life because it determines the direction of heat transfer. Heat always flows from a body at a higher temperature to one at a lower temperature.

There are three common temperature scales: Celsius (°C), Fahrenheit (°F), and Kelvin (K). The Celsius scale is based on the freezing and boiling points of water, which are 0°C and 100°C respectively. The Fahrenheit scale sets the freezing point of water at 32°F and the boiling point at 212°F. The Kelvin scale, used primarily in scientific contexts, starts at absolute zero, the theoretical point where all molecular motion stops, which is 0 K, equivalent to -273.15°C.

Temperature is a fundamental parameter in thermodynamics, a branch of physics that deals with heat and temperature and their relation to energy and work. The laws of thermodynamics describe how energy moves and changes form. The Zeroth Law of Thermodynamics states that if two systems are each in thermal equilibrium with a third system, they are in thermal equilibrium with each other, which allows for the definition of temperature.

Methods to Measure Temperature

  • Thermometers: The most common instruments for measuring temperature, thermometers can be liquid-in-glass (mercury or alcohol), digital, or infrared.
  • Thermocouples: These are widely used in industrial applications. A thermocouple consists of two different types of metals joined together at one end. When the junction of the two metals is heated or cooled, it produces a voltage that can be correlated back to temperature.
  • Infrared Sensors: These measure the infrared radiation emitted by an object. They are useful for measuring temperature from a distance, especially in situations where a traditional thermometer cannot be used.
  • Resistance Temperature Detectors (RTDs): These devices measure temperature by correlating the resistance of the RTD element with temperature. They are known for their accuracy and stability over a wide temperature range.
  • Thermistors: Similar to RTDs, thermistors are temperature-sensitive resistors. They are made from ceramic materials that exhibit a large change in resistance with temperature, making them very sensitive.
  • Bimetallic Strips: Used in some thermostats, these consist of two different metals bonded together that expand at different rates as they are heated. This causes the strip to bend, and this bending can be used to measure temperature or activate switches.
  • Liquid Crystal Temperature Sensors: These contain liquid crystals that change color based on temperature. They are often used in medical thermometers and other applications where visual temperature indication is useful.

Temperature Measuring Scales

  • Fahrenheit (°F)
